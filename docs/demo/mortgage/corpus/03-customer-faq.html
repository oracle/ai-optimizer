Mortgage Customer FAQ

Plain-language answers to the questions our mortgage customers ask most often. If you cannot find an answer here, please call us at the number on your monthly statement.

Payments and escrow

When is my payment due? Your monthly payment is due on the first of each month. We do not charge a late fee until the 16th, but interest continues to accrue from the first.

What is included in my monthly payment? Most mortgages include four components — principal, interest, property taxes, and homeowners insurance. The tax and insurance portions go into an escrow account and are paid out by the bank when those bills come due.

Why did my monthly payment change even though I have a fixed-rate mortgage? Your interest rate is fixed, but property taxes and insurance premiums change over time. When they go up, your escrow shortfall is spread across the next twelve payments. When they go down, you receive a refund and your payment drops.

Can I make extra principal payments? Yes. There is no penalty for making extra principal payments at any time. Make sure to mark the extra payment as "principal-only" so it is not applied to next month's payment instead.

Prepayment and payoff

Is there a penalty for paying off my mortgage early? It depends on the product:

  • 30-Year Fixed Prime (FIXED30-PRIME): No penalty after the first 36 months. A small penalty (1% of remaining principal) applies if you pay off in full during the first 36 months.
  • 5/1 Adjustable-Rate Standard (ARM5_1-STD): 1% of remaining principal in the first 24 months, then no penalty.
  • 15-Year Jumbo Prime (JUMBO15-PRIME): No penalty at any time.
  • 30-Year FHA (FHA-30YR): No prepayment penalty.

How do I get a payoff statement? Request one through the customer portal or by calling. The statement is good for 30 days and shows the exact amount needed to pay off your loan, including per-day interest.

Refinancing

When does it make sense to refinance? A common rule of thumb is when the new rate would be at least 0.75% lower than your current rate, and you plan to stay in the home long enough to recover the closing costs (typically 2–5% of the loan amount).

Will I have to re-qualify? Yes. A refinance is a brand-new loan, so we will pull a current credit report and verify current income, employment, and reserves. A borrower who qualified for a particular product years ago may no longer qualify for the same product today if their credit, income, or property value has changed.

Can I shorten my term when I refinance? Yes. Many customers refinance from a 30-year into a 15-year mortgage to save on total interest. The monthly payment is higher, so make sure the new payment fits comfortably in your budget.

Hardship programs

I lost my job. What can I do? Call us as soon as possible — do not wait until you have missed a payment. We offer three hardship options depending on the situation:

  1. Forbearance: A temporary pause or reduction of monthly payments for up to six months. Missed payments are added to the end of the loan or repaid on a schedule.
  2. Loan modification: A permanent change to the loan terms — typically a lower interest rate or a longer term — to reduce the monthly payment.
  3. Repayment plan: Spread missed payments across the next 6–12 months in addition to your regular payment.

Hardship programs do affect your credit report, but they affect it far less than a foreclosure would.

Will my credit be hurt by a hardship program? Forbearance and modifications are reported to the credit bureaus. The exact impact depends on the program, your prior payment history, and the bureau. Talking to us early gives you more options than waiting until you have already missed payments.

Property taxes and insurance

Why did my escrow account go up so much this year? The two most common reasons are a property reassessment that raised your tax bill, or an insurance premium increase. The bank does not control either of these — we collect what is owed and pay it on your behalf. You will receive an annual escrow analysis statement showing exactly how the new amount was calculated.

Can I cancel escrow and pay taxes and insurance myself? On conventional loans (not FHA), you may request to cancel escrow once your loan-to-value ratio drops below 80% and you have a clean payment history for the prior twelve months. FHA loans require escrow for the life of the loan.
